Proposition: Abuse of Power is the most common complaint

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Descriptitve titles such as “Most Common Complaint Types”
  • Minial detail on other categories doesn’t give the reader insight into whether other categories are growing over time
  • Omitted irrelevant outlier data to maintain focus

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Used red tones to suggest danger
  • Excluding “Abuse of Authority” from the plot entirely hides the dominant category completely to mislead the viewer.
  • Equal bar colors/spacing implies equal weight or importance, even when counts differ.

Final Reflection

Throughout this project, I found it surprisingly easy to influence viewers’ perceptions simply by changing how the data was framed. Creating persuasive visualizations in support of the proposition — that "Abuse of Authority is the most common complaint" — was fairly straightforward: highlighting raw counts and dominant proportions naturally told that story. However, designing visualizations to oppose the proposition was more challenging. It required more deliberate choices, such as filtering data, adjusting chart types, or reordering categories, to divert attention away from the dominant narrative.

One thing that surprised me was how subtle changes, like omitting a single category or adjusting the color palette, could meaningfully shift the viewer’s interpretation without necessarily falsifying the data. These small decisions, although technically accurate, had major persuasive effects.

After completing this project, I now view "ethical visualization" as a balance between storytelling and responsibility. While all visualizations involve framing decisions, ethical visualization requires maintaining transparency about what is shown — and what is left out. This project emphasized how easy it is to unintentionally or intentionally mislead an audience, reinforcing the critical need for both creators and viewers to approach data visualizations thoughtfully and skeptically.
